Domain 5 - Incident Handling Video

- Incident Handling
		○ Despite even the best plans, chances are an attack will eventually succeed
			§ The damage that an organisation receives from said attack often depends on how well the organisation was prepared
		○ Many security resources and security product vendors recommend security-related data collection and analysis at scale to stay ahead of attackers
			§ Even for small networks, collecting, organising, analysing, and responding to the sheer volume of activity information across a network is nearly impossible without the help of sophisticated collection automation software
			§ Security information and event management (SIEM) systems and security orchestration automation and response (SOAR) systems provide the advanced functionality needed to handle the large volume of security-related activity information within an IT infrastructure
				□ Maintaining the security of any environment requires systems with the sophistication of SIEM or SOAR products 
	• SIEM System - An integrated collection of software products and services
		○ Configured to collect real-time performance data from computers and devices across a network
		○ Organises and analyses streams of input data and isolates any activity that appears to be suspicious
		○ Helps security management create reports and triggers alerts for suspected security incidents
		○ Configuring and managing SIEM systems is an ongoing task
			§ Balancing the sensitivity of system response with the number or alerts generated
		○ Can allow security professionals to focus on the most important event within an IT infrastructure when tuned properly
	• SOAR System - A newer type of security software and service package
		○ Extends the functionality of a SIEM system
		○ Collects security information from a wide range of inputs, organises and analyses data and helps to manage the response to any alerts
		○ Incorporates orchestration and response actives in the event and incident response process
			§ The systems include documentation capabilities as well as project management and workflow aspects
		○ Helps cybersecurity professionals identify, classify, and respond to security incidents as effectively as possible 
	• SIEM and SOAR systems both build on industry best practices and incorporate data analysis and automation to respond more quickly and with fewer resources
		○ To keep environments safe
- Identifying Security Incidents
	• Regardless of the diligence of security professionals and the sophistication of the techniques they employ, it is likely that sooner or later, a security incident will occur
	• Security Incident - Any event that results in a violation of security policy or an impending violation of security policy 
	• Event - Normal actions that occur in the day-to-day operation of IT infrastructure 
		○ Events may include accounts signing in, requesting access to resources, and consuming services
		○ A security incident would be any event that results in an unauthorised action
	• The only way to know whether a security incident has occurred in to constantly monitor events and determine if any detected events violate the security policy
		○ There are three steps in the detection process:
			1. Event information must be collected
			2. Event information must be analysed
			3. The results of the analysis must be examined to determine if a specific event satisfies the criteria for a security incident
	• The data most commonly examined for evidence of security incidents represents the activity of subjects in an IT infrastructure 
		○ Data of interest can include packet captures of network traffic, log files for applications, operating systems, and devices, as well as any alerts generated by security devices or other network components
	• All of these data sources can provide important input to the security incident identification process
		○ Some attacks such as data exfiltration, may occur with little or no evidence of the incident
		○ It is only by monitoring network data that security incidents can be reliably be identified by learning to recognise the difference between normal and abnormal
	• Suspicious Events - An action that occurs in an IT infrastructure that deviates from normal behaviour 
		○ It is important to study normal behaviour, as this makes it easy to recognise the behaviour that is not normal
			§ Most ddos attacks are obvious to the most casual observe
				□ This is because they create unavailable services which cause operation disruption. This attracts a lot of attention
			§ On the other hand, an attack that is targeted at accessing confidential information may not be discovered very easily
				□ In the case of attacks such as this, it will often take weeks or even months for organisations to determine that an incident has occurred
				□ The only way to shorten the time between action and discovery is to actively look for suspicious behaviour
					® Train observers on the appearance of normal behaviour 
						◊ Observers may be security personnel or machine learning algorithms
						◊ The observer needs to understand what normal behaviour looks like
							} Only then can that observer raise an alert when examining behaviour that deviates from the definition of normal
						◊ Wireshark can be used to detect malicious activity
			§ The only way to consistently identify suspicious behaviour is to engage automation and advanced algorithms to examine near real-time activity continually 
				□ In this way, it is much more likely to identify suspicious activity early in the attack process
					® This gives security professionals more time to react, contains potential damage, and could theoretically prevent a new incident from becoming a large scale attack
- Attack Frameworks - Cyberattacks do not occur randomly, they generally follow a similar sequence of events; an attack framework is a formal study of these events created to help cybersecurity professionals better understand how attacks work
	• Cyber Kill Chain Framework
		1. Reconnaissance - Harvesting email addresses, conference information, etc.
		2. Weaponization - Coupling exploit with backdoor into deliverable payload 
		3. Delivery - Delivering the weaponized bundle to the victim via email, web, USB, etc.
		4. Exploitation - Exploiting a vulnerability to executed code on the victim's systems
		5. Installation - Installing malware on the asset
		6. Command & Control - Command channel for the remote manipulation of the victim
		7. Action On Objectives - With 'Hand on Keyboard' access, intruders accomplish their original goals
	• The MITRE ATTACK framework separates tactics for enterprise and mobile attacks
	• The DTIC (Defence Technical Information Center) is responsible for the diamond model
- Tactics Techniques and Procedures (TTP) 
	• The behaviour of an actor. A tactic is the highest-level description of the behaviour; while techniques provide a more detailed description of the behaviour in the context of a tactic; and procedures provide a lower level, highly detailed description of the behaviour in the context of a technique
	• In other words: 
		○ Tactic - Generally describes an action or some behaviour
		○ Technique - Provides the 'how they did it' description
		○ Procedure - Details of steps to carry out the specific technique
- Evidence Sources and Handling
	• Evidence is the supporting information that helps determine what happened
		○ Evidence may simply shed light on some event, or it may be what law enforcement or a court requires to make a change or render a verdict
		○ Identifying, collecting, and protecting evidence is of utmost importance to protect the integrity of investigations
	• When targeting an IT infrastructure, the best evidence is any original documents
		○ This includes supporting data contained in its original file collected from its original location
	• Secondary Evidence is any evidence that is a copy of the original 
		○ Could be backup images, replicated storage locations, or locations people or processes have copied from the original location
	• In some cases evidence can be physical, such as a disk drive, router, or USB
		○ When physical evidence is evolved, it is important that the collection and analysis process does not change the contents of those devices 
	• Artifact - Every piece of evidence is referred to as an artifact
		○ A court will require a demonstration that the provenance of the evidence is clear
			§ A clear record of how the evidence was identified, collected, and protected to ensure that it stayed in the same state as when it was originally created
			§ The process is often referred to as chain of custody
	• Chain of Custody
		○ Ensure that digital evidence remain in the same format and state as when it was collected
		○ Special care must be taken when collecting electronic devices and digital evidence to disable any potential for writing to or altering the state of the evidence
		○ Digital forensics investigators commonly use specially designed software and hardware to ensure that images of data are collected bit by bit with no alterations
	• Hashing
		○ Commonly used to demonstrate integrity of evidence
		○ One of the first steps in a digital investigation is often to create a hash value of a file or volume
			§ The desired evidence is then copied in a nonlathering manner for future analysis
			§ If the calculated hash value matches the value of the original hash value, then this supports the integrity of this artifact
	• Although protecting the chain of custody is labour-intensive and slows down investigations;
		○ It is always better to assume that collected evidence will end up in a court of law, since sloppy collection procedures cannot be cleaned up later
		○ Evidence altered or damaged through its collection or improper handling will almost definitely not be admissible to a court of law
- Compliance Frameworks
	• The entire reason for handling incidents is to respond in a structured manner to a violation or threatened violation of security policy
		○ Security policy is in place to protect your organisation
		○ Violations of security policy have consequences
			§ These range from minor disruptions to catastrophic impacts on business operations
		○ Security incidents do not always result in outages
			§ In some cases, organisations are mandated to comply with standards or regulations, and violations could result in substantial penalties or even legal action
	• It is imperative that security professionals understand the mandates that their organisation must operate within
		○ Ensure that incidents do not violate those mandates
	• Several of the most important mandates that affect the operation of covered entities include expansive regulations and agreed-upon industry standards
		○ The GDPR of the European Union protects consumer privacy for all EU citizens
		○ Violations of GDPR carry stiff penalties which can be punitive to any organisation
	• HIPPA
		○ US law that places restrictions on how personal health information us processed, stored, and transmitted
		○ As with GDPR, violations of HIPPA carry stiff penalties 
	• PCI DSS
		○ Industry agreed-upon standard
			§ Not a law
		○ An agreement made by the largest payment processors
			§ They thus avoided a standard mandates by a legislative body
		○ PCI DSS is now compulsory for any organisation that deals with payment cards
		○ Violation of PCI DSS not only results in financial loss, but also the potential loss of the ability to accept payment cards
			§ This can have very large and very bad effects on businesses that rely on payment cards for transactions
	• FERPA
		○ Protects the privacy of student educational records
		○ Applies to any and all education institutions that receive federal funding
		○ States that educational records can only be accessed by the student or a narrowly defined entity (such as the administrative staff)
		○ Parents can access the educational records for the child, but not after the child turns 18
		○ Organisations that violate FERPA risk losing the federal funding 
	• FISMA
		○ Sets standards for federal information systems that are not related to national security
		○ Violating these standards result in sanctions that negatively impact an agency's ability to carry out its requirements
	• It is essential to understand the compliance requirements that you must comply to, and the consequences for not doing so.
- Reporting 
	• While it way be embarrassing to publicly show when there is a security incident (due to customers or worse, potential customs finding out), some regulations or laws require that incidents be disclosed in certain situations
		○ It is important for security personnel to be familiar with requirements that mandate incident notification and reporting to avoid penalties for noncompliance 
	• Whether or not an incident must be reported can depend on:
		○ Geographic location
		○ Nature of operations
		○ Nature and scope of incident
	• GDPR 
		○ All organisations must report certain types of personal information breaches within 72 hours
	• HIPPA
		○ BNR (Breach Notification Rule) requires a risk assessment to determine if notification of a breach is required
	• In today's environment, any security incident that results in a sizeable disclosure of data requires notification
		○ It is the responsibility of each organisation to determine what type of incidents fall under notification requirements
